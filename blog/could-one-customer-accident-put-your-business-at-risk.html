General Liability

Could One Customer Accident Put Your Business at Risk?

A single customer slip-and-fall in your parking lot — or a defective product that hurts someone in their own kitchen — can trigger a six-figure claim that drains reserves and closes the doors of a small Lowcountry business.

April 9, 2026 6 min read Goose Creek, SC

If you own a small business in Berkeley County — a coffee shop on Red Bank Road, a contractor based out of Goose Creek, a boutique on St. James Avenue — one customer accident can be the difference between an annual policy premium and bankruptcy. General liability insurance exists for that exact moment.

What general liability actually covers

"General liability" is a deceptively simple name for one of the most important policies a business buys. It is the coverage that responds when your operations cause harm to a third party — a customer, a vendor, a passerby, anyone who isn't an employee or you. It pays for four distinct categories of loss:

  • Bodily injury — medical bills, ambulance charges, surgery, rehab, and pain-and-suffering settlements when someone is physically hurt by your business.
  • Property damage — repair or replacement when your work damages a customer's home, vehicle, or possessions.
  • Personal & advertising injury — the lesser-known piece. Libel, slander, copyright infringement in your ads, false-arrest claims tied to a shoplifting incident.
  • Legal defense costs — attorneys, court fees, expert witnesses, and depositions, paid in addition to the limit on most policies.

What a "small" claim really costs

A slip-and-fall in a wet entryway sounds minor. The numbers say otherwise. The average general-liability slip-and-fall claim that goes to trial settles for $30,000 – $50,000, and that's before legal defense. Add a broken hip, a back surgery, or six months of physical therapy and you are easily into six figures. We see these claims monthly.

A real Lowcountry example

A Berkeley County coffee shop owner called us after a customer tripped on a curled floor mat. Initial ER visit: $4,200. Plaintiff's attorney sent a demand letter six weeks later: $187,000. The carrier's defense team negotiated a $42,000 settlement and absorbed roughly $18,000 in legal fees. Total out-of-pocket for the owner: the policy deductible.

Three exposures most owners forget

1. Off-premises operations

If you do any work outside your storefront — a caterer dropping off platters, an HVAC tech in a customer's house, a fitness instructor running an outdoor class — that work is covered too, but only if the policy is written correctly. Make sure the application reflects every job site, not just your business address.

2. Product liability

Anything you make, sell, distribute, or even just resell carries product-liability exposure. A bakery that sells gluten-free cookies and accidentally cross-contaminates with peanut oil has product-liability exposure. Most general liability policies fold this in under "products and completed operations" — verify that the limit is adequate and not sublimited.

3. Hired and non-owned auto

This isn't general liability per se, but it lives next door to it. If an employee runs to the bank in their personal car for you, and they cause a wreck, your business can be named in the lawsuit. A simple endorsement closes that gap for under $200/year on most policies.

The umbrella conversation

Once your business has assets — equipment, inventory, real estate, accounts receivable, a brand worth protecting — a $1M general-liability limit can be exhausted in a single bad claim. A commercial umbrella sits on top of GL, commercial auto, and employer's liability and adds $1M, $2M, or $5M of additional protection for a few hundred to a few thousand dollars a year. For most small businesses, it is the cheapest seven figures of coverage you will ever buy.

Quick gut-check

If a customer sued your business tomorrow for $1.2M, would your current policy cover it — and would you still own your home, your vehicles, and your savings when the dust settled? If you hesitate, that's the conversation we want to have.

What to do this week

  1. Pull your declarations page. Look at "Each Occurrence" and "General Aggregate." Are they at least $1M / $2M?
  2. Check the schedule of operations. Make sure every service you actually perform is listed and rated.
  3. Verify the additional-insured endorsements. Landlords, prime contractors, and event venues should be listed if your contracts require it.
  4. Ask about an umbrella. If you don't have one, get a quote — they are usually cheaper than expected.
