Full-Time RVing: Coverage Gaps to Watch For

If your motorhome or fifth wheel is your home — not a vacation toy — your insurance needs to behave like a homeowners policy with wheels. Most stock RV policies don't.

February 19, 2026 7 min read Moncks Corner, SC

A standard RV policy is priced and underwritten on the assumption that the rig is parked in your driveway 50 weeks a year and used for a couple of long weekends and one big summer trip. If you're full-timing — six months a year or more — that math falls apart, and the policy quietly stops covering things you assume it does.

What a "weekend" RV policy actually covers

  • Liability while driving (motorhomes) or while attached to a tow vehicle (trailers).
  • Collision and comprehensive on the rig.
  • A small contents limit — typically $3,000 to $5,000.
  • Roadside assistance and emergency expenses (capped low — usually $750).
  • Limited liability while parked at a campsite (usually requires the unit to be set up).

What a full-timer endorsement adds

Full-timer coverage rewrites the policy to function like a homeowners policy when the rig is your residence:

A real coverage gap

A retired couple sold their Summerville home and moved into a 38' Class A. Two months later a guest tripped over their patio mat at the campsite and broke a wrist. The claim — $34,000 — was denied because the standard policy excluded liability for "extended stays" beyond 30 days at one site. A full-timer endorsement at $180/year would have covered it in full.

Three traps full-timers walk into

1. Domicile vs. residence confusion

Where you "live" for tax and license purposes (your domicile state) often differs from where you're parked. Insurance follows the registration state. Pick a domicile state with reasonable RV rates (SC, FL, TX are common) and keep registration, driver's license, and policy aligned.

2. Toad / tow-vehicle coverage

If you flat-tow a Jeep behind your motorhome, the Jeep needs its own auto policy with liability while being towed and physical damage coverage that doesn't exclude towed-vehicle damage. Many auto policies do.

3. Inland-marine for "the stuff that lives in there"

Cameras, laptops, e-bikes, fishing gear, jewelry — the high-value items inside a full-timer's rig often exceed the personal-property sublimits. Schedule them on an inland-marine endorsement (or your separate homeowners contents floater if you've kept a sticks-and-bricks).

Disclose full-time use

If you tell your carrier "occasional use" and they later discover you've been full-timing, they can deny the claim or rescind the policy from inception. Be honest at application — it's a $150 – $300/year difference in premium and a five-figure difference at claim time.

Endorsements worth the cost

  • Total-loss replacement — for the first 5 years, totaled rig is replaced with a new comparable model, no depreciation. Common on units under $200K.
  • Mexico coverage — if you're snowbirding to Baja, this is non-optional.
  • Pet injury coverage — pays for vet bills if your dog is hurt in a covered accident.
  • Roadside with RV-specific service — most auto roadside services don't cover anything over 10,000 lbs. Get RV-specific.
