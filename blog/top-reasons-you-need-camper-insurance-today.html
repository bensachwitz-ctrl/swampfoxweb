Camper / RV

Top Reasons You Need Camper Insurance Today

If you tow a travel trailer, fifth wheel, or pop-up — your auto policy doesn't cover the trailer once it's detached, and your homeowners doesn't follow it on the road. A dedicated camper policy is the only thing that does.

January 20, 2026 6 min read Summerville, SC

"My truck's policy covers the trailer, right?" It's the most common assumption we hear, and it is wrong about 80% of the time. Here's exactly when your auto policy covers a camper, when it doesn't, and what a real camper policy adds.

What your auto policy actually does

Most personal auto policies extend liability only to a trailer while it's attached to the towing vehicle. That means: if the trailer comes unhitched and rolls into another car, the auto liability pays for the other car. Physical damage to the trailer itself is excluded on virtually every standard auto policy.

The moment you unhitch — at the campsite, in the storage lot, anywhere — even the liability extension stops. Now the trailer is uncovered.

Five things only a camper policy covers

1. Comprehensive on the trailer itself

Hail, theft, fire, vandalism, fallen trees at the campsite, deer collisions while towing, water damage from a roof leak. These are the most common camper losses and none of them are covered by your auto policy.

2. Collision on the trailer

Backed it into a tree at the campground? Took a corner too sharp and scraped a fence? Hit by an uninsured pickup in a parking lot? Collision pays. Auto policies don't cover this damage to the camper.

3. Personal property inside

Camping gear, kitchenware, bedding, electronics, tools, fishing equipment — typically $3,000 – $20,000 of contents coverage at replacement cost. Your homeowners covers personal property "anywhere in the world," but with a low off-premises sublimit (often 10%) and your homeowners deductible.

4. Personal liability while parked

Once the trailer is set up at a campsite, your auto liability is gone. If a guest trips on your awning rope, you have no commercial-style liability coverage unless you have a camper policy with vacation-liability included.

5. Roadside assistance for trailers

Most auto roadside services explicitly exclude trailers. RV-specific roadside (typically $25 – $50/year) covers trailer tire changes, flatbed tows, and on-site mechanical service.

A real claim from last summer

A Berkeley County family parked a 24' travel trailer behind their house for the off-season. A pine limb came through the roof during a thunderstorm. Damage: $7,400. Their homeowners denied because the trailer was titled separately and not on the schedule. Their auto policy denied because the trailer was unhitched. The dedicated camper policy they wished they had would have cost $260/year.

Endorsements that punch above their weight

  • Total-loss replacement — first 5 model years, totaled trailer replaced new with the same model. Adds 5 – 10% to premium.
  • Permanent attachments — awnings, satellite dishes, solar panels, slide-toppers. Without this, they're depreciated heavily at claim time.
  • Vacation liability — covers personal liability while parked at any campsite, not just designated ones.
  • Emergency expense — pays for hotel, food, transportation if your trailer is uninhabitable mid-trip.
Bundle for the discount

Most carriers offer a 5 – 10% multi-policy discount when the camper sits on the same account as your home and auto. Worth asking — you usually pay back the cost of the camper policy in bundle savings on the others.
