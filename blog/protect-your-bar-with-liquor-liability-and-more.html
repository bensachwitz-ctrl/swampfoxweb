Protect Your Bar with Liquor Liability and More

Standard general liability has a one-line exclusion that will close your bar overnight: it does not cover anything related to serving alcohol. Here's the policy stack any establishment serving liquor in South Carolina actually needs.

December 17, 2025 7 min read Charleston, SC

South Carolina is a "dram shop" state. That means a bar, restaurant, or event host who serves alcohol to a visibly intoxicated person — or to anyone underage — can be held legally responsible for what that person does after they leave. Drunk-driving deaths, assaults, property damage. The lawsuit names the establishment, and the standard general-liability policy explicitly excludes the entire category.

The exclusion that bites

Open any commercial general-liability policy and find the section labeled "Liquor Liability Exclusion." It typically reads: "This insurance does not apply to bodily injury or property damage for which any insured may be held liable by reason of: causing or contributing to the intoxication of any person; the furnishing of alcoholic beverages to a person under the legal drinking age or under the influence of alcohol..."

If your business is "in the business of" serving, manufacturing, distributing, or selling alcohol, that exclusion eliminates coverage for the largest single category of claims you face.

The four-policy stack for alcohol-serving businesses

1. Commercial General Liability (GL) — $1M / $2M minimum

Slip-and-falls, property damage, premises liability — all the non-alcohol exposures.

2. Liquor Liability — $1M minimum

Responds when an intoxicated patron causes harm to themselves or a third party after being over-served. Required for any business that serves alcohol on-premises. Typical premium: $800 – $4,000/year depending on revenue mix and licensing.

3. Assault & Battery coverage

Often excluded from standard GL and liquor liability. Bar fights, parking-lot incidents, security-staff use of force — A&B coverage adds it back. For any establishment open past 11 p.m., this is non-negotiable. Limits typically sublimit at $50K – $500K per occurrence.

4. Commercial Property + Business Income

Building, contents, liquor inventory (which has its own valuation rules — replacement value of a wine cellar can run six figures), and business income to keep payroll going during a covered shutdown.

Real liability under SC dram-shop case law

South Carolina courts have repeatedly held bars and restaurants liable for the actions of patrons who were over-served. Recent settlements and verdicts in the Charleston area have ranged from $300,000 for a single-vehicle DUI fatality involving a patron up to $2.4M for a multi-victim crash. Without liquor liability, those numbers come straight out of business assets and personal guarantees.

Loss-prevention practices that lower premium

  • TIPS or ServSafe Alcohol training for every server and bartender, documented and dated. Most carriers will give a 5 – 15% credit for full-staff certification.
  • Written cut-off policy with documented procedures for refusing service and cab/rideshare assistance.
  • Camera coverage over service areas, parking lot, and entrances. Footage often defeats fraudulent claims.
  • ID-scanner at the door for any establishment that admits guests under 30. Provable due diligence on age verification is a powerful defense.
  • Posted "no last-minute" rule — typically no shots in the last 30 minutes before closing.

Less-obvious exposures bars and restaurants miss

  • Liquor inventory valuation — high-end wine and spirits should be scheduled. Standard contents coverage may pay actual cash value rather than replacement cost.
  • Spoilage coverage — power outage takes out walk-in coolers; food and draft beer loss adds up fast.
  • Equipment breakdown — ice machines, walk-ins, exhaust hoods, draft systems.
  • Employment Practices Liability (EPLI) — restaurants and bars are statistically among the most frequent EPLI claimants, especially around hiring, harassment, and tip-pooling disputes.
  • Cyber liability — POS breaches in hospitality are common; coverage is now affordable for small operators.
One-time event hosts: don't skip this

Hosting a wedding, fundraiser, or company party where alcohol is served? Even a one-night event creates dram-shop exposure. Special-event liquor liability policies cost $200 – $600 for the night and are required by most reputable venues.
