Black Ice Accidents: What Auto Insurance Really Covers

Lowcountry bridges freeze before the road does. The Don Holt, the Cooper River, the Wando — every winter we get the same calls from drivers who slid into a guardrail at 4 a.m. Here's exactly which coverage on your policy actually pays.

March 26, 2026 6 min read Summerville, SC

Black ice doesn't look like ice. That's the whole problem. The film is thin enough that the asphalt shows through, and a driver hits it at highway speed without ever touching the brakes. The wreck that follows is almost always single-vehicle, almost always at night or just before sunrise, and almost always on a bridge.

The "single-vehicle slide" — collision, not comprehensive

Drivers assume that because there was no other car involved, comprehensive should pay. It does not. Comprehensive covers losses that aren't the result of impact — theft, vandalism, hail, fire, glass, animal strikes. The moment your vehicle hits the guardrail, the median, or anything else, it becomes a collision claim.

This matters for two reasons:

  • Deductibles often differ. Comprehensive deductibles are typically lower ($250 – $500). Collision deductibles run higher ($500 – $1,000). Black ice triggers the higher one.
  • Surcharges are different. A comprehensive claim usually doesn't raise rates. A collision claim — even when the road is the obvious cause — can.

A real Lowcountry slide

A Summerville driver hit black ice on the Ashley River bridge at 5:50 a.m. and slid into the concrete barrier. Vehicle damage: $14,800. Medical: $3,400. Guardrail repair billed by SCDOT: $4,200. The driver's collision coverage with $1,000 deductible paid the vehicle, MedPay covered the ER visit, and liability paid the state. Total out-of-pocket: $1,000. Without collision coverage, that number would have been $14,800.

The "phantom vehicle" loophole

If another driver caused you to swerve onto black ice — they cut you off, brake-checked you, ran you into a barrier — and they fled the scene, you may have a phantom-vehicle UM claim. South Carolina allows UM coverage to apply even without contact between vehicles, provided the incident is reported within 48 hours and there is independent corroboration (witness, dashcam, traffic-cam footage). This converts a "your fault" collision into a "covered by UM" loss with a different deductible structure.

What to do in the first 30 minutes

  1. Get out of the lane safely. Lowcountry bridges have minimal shoulder. Hazard lights on, exit the vehicle on the side away from traffic.
  2. Call 911 even for a single-vehicle slide. A police report is the difference between a fast claim and a months-long argument.
  3. Photograph everything. Vehicle, road surface, the bridge, weather conditions. Black ice will be gone in two hours and there will be no other proof it existed.
  4. Call us, not the 800 number. If you're a Swamp Fox client, call the office. We open the claim with the carrier, stay in the loop, and advocate when the adjuster tries to apply the wrong deductible.
Don't admit fault at the scene

Telling the responding officer "I was going too fast" can lock you into a comparative-negligence finding that hurts a UM claim later. Stick to facts: speed, lane, what you observed. Your insurance company decides fault, not you.

The policy review we recommend before next winter

  • Carry collision and comprehensive if your vehicle is worth more than ~$3,500. Without them, a black-ice slide is a total out-of-pocket loss.
  • Match your UM/UIM limits to your liability limits. If you have $100/300 BI liability, carry $100/300 UM/UIM. More on UM/UIM →
  • Add roadside and rental reimbursement. Combined cost is usually under $80/year and they pay for themselves the first time.
  • Consider raising your collision deductible to $1,000 if you have $1,000+ in savings. The premium savings often beat the deductible difference within 18 months.
