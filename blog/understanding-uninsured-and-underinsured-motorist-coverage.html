Understanding Uninsured & Underinsured Motorist Coverage

If you're hit by someone with no insurance — or not enough — your own UM and UIM coverage is what stands between you and the bill. In South Carolina, this is one coverage you should not minimize.

October 21, 2025 7 min read Charleston, SC

South Carolina is among the worst states in the country for uninsured drivers. Estimates put about 1 in 8 drivers carrying zero insurance, and a much larger share carrying only the state minimum of 25/50/25 — barely enough to cover an emergency-room visit. When one of them hits you, your protection comes from your own policy.

Two coverages, one purpose

Uninsured Motorist (UM) pays your bodily injury and property damage when the at-fault driver has no insurance, fled the scene (hit and run), or was driving a stolen vehicle. SC requires every auto policy to carry at least 25/50/25 in UM unless the insured signs a written rejection.

Underinsured Motorist (UIM) pays the gap when the at-fault driver has insurance but not enough to cover your damages. UIM is optional in SC but inexpensive, and it's where most large claims actually land.

How they pay — a real-world example

Scenario: rear-ended on I-26

You're rear-ended at a stop on I-26. ER visit, two surgeries, six weeks of lost wages. Total damages: $210,000. The at-fault driver carries SC minimum 25/50/25 — their insurance pays $25,000. You're left with a $185,000 gap.

If you carry $250,000 UIM, your own policy pays the remaining $185,000 (because their $25K + your UIM up to $250K = $275K total available).

If you carry only minimum 25/25 UIM, your policy pays nothing extra (the at-fault driver already met your floor) and the $185,000 comes out of your savings, your wages, and your home equity.

South Carolina's "stacking" rule

SC is a "stacking" state. If you have multiple vehicles on a single policy or multiple policies in the same household, your UM/UIM limits can stack — multiplying your effective coverage:

  • Two vehicles, $100K UIM each, stacking = $200K available per claim.
  • Three vehicles, $100K UIM each, stacking = $300K available per claim.

Stacking typically costs an extra 10 – 25% on the UM/UIM line — usually $30 – $80/year per vehicle — and the math is overwhelming for households with two or more cars. Always ask whether your policy is stacked.

The "phantom vehicle" rule

South Carolina UM coverage applies even without contact between vehicles, provided three conditions are met:

  1. You report the incident to police within 48 hours.
  2. You file a sworn statement that an unidentified vehicle caused the loss.
  3. An independent witness corroborates the phantom vehicle (dashcam, traffic camera, third-party witness).

This converts what would otherwise be a single-vehicle "your fault" loss into a UM-covered claim. Worth knowing the next time someone runs you off Highway 17.

Three details that change a claim

1. UM/UIM follows the person, not the car

If you're injured as a pedestrian, on a bicycle, or as a passenger in someone else's car, your UM/UIM still applies. This is one of the most underused features of personal auto insurance.

2. UM/UIM is paid by your own carrier — but it's adversarial

Even though it's "your" coverage, your carrier will negotiate your UM/UIM claim like any third-party claim. Have an attorney review any settlement offer above $25K. Most personal-injury lawyers handle UM/UIM cases on contingency.

3. Don't sign a release before exhausting all coverage

If the at-fault driver's carrier offers a quick settlement at their policy limit, get a written consent-to-settle from your own UIM carrier before signing. Settling without consent can void the UIM coverage entirely.

The single-line policy review

Pull your declarations page. Find the "UIM" line. Compare it to the "Bodily Injury Liability" line just above. If UIM is lower than your liability, you've out-protected the other driver from causing harm to you. Match them. It's almost always worth it.
