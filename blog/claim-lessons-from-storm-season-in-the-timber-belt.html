Forestry Equipment

Claim Lessons from Storm Season in the Timber Belt

After every hurricane season, we sit down with operators who filed equipment claims and ask what worked, what didn't, and what they wish they'd done differently. The pattern repeats every year. Here's the playbook for the next one.

December 10, 2025 8 min read SC Timber Belt

Storm season in the Timber Belt — the swath of pine and hardwood country running from coastal Carolina inland — has produced a remarkably consistent claims pattern over the last several years. Skidders parked under tree cover get crushed. Lowboys and trailers blow over when not lowered. Tools and laptops left in service trucks get water-damaged. Below: what the policies actually paid, where they fell short, and the loss-prevention steps that move the needle.

Lesson 1 — Where you park matters more than what you parked

The single largest equipment loss we've seen in the last three years was a $480,000 grapple skidder destroyed by a 70-foot loblolly pine. The skidder had been moved to "shelter" under tree cover ahead of the storm. The shelter became the projectile.

What the policy paid: full replacement under the inland-marine schedule, less the $5,000 deductible. The carrier did not contest the claim, but a structured loss-prevention program would have moved the equipment to open ground 200 feet from the tree line.

Pre-storm equipment placement

Move heavy equipment to designated open-ground staging at least 200 feet from any tree line. Park in a tight cluster, oriented nose into the wind. Lower booms, blades, and grapples. Disconnect batteries on units that won't run for several days.

Lesson 2 — Lowboys and float trailers blow over

A surprising number of forestry equipment losses involve trailers — not the equipment loaded on them. Empty lowboys with their decks at standard height present a sail surface to crosswind. We've seen multiple total losses where a 50' lowboy flipped onto its side overnight, taking out chains, binders, and side rails.

Pre-storm protocol: drop the deck to the lowest setting, chock all wheels, and where possible, load a heavy item (a stack of mats, a bin of chains) to lower the center of gravity.

Lesson 3 — The contents-of-truck claim everyone overlooks

Service trucks often hold $15,000 – $40,000 of tools, diagnostic equipment, parts inventory, and a laptop. Standard commercial auto covers the truck. It does not cover the contents.

After a storm with broken windows and roof leaks, we routinely see contents losses ignored because the operator assumed they weren't covered. A properly endorsed contractor's tools and equipment coverage (inland marine) pays for these — but only if the policy carries it. Confirm now, not after the storm.

Lesson 4 — The 72-hour business-interruption waiting period

Standard business interruption coverage carries a 72-hour deductible — meaning the carrier pays for lost income only after the third day of suspended operations. After a major storm, most logging operations are down for one to two weeks. The first three days are out of pocket.

For forestry-specific operations, we can often negotiate this down to 48 or 24 hours. Worth asking — the per-day cost of a downed crew can run into thousands.

Lesson 5 — Documentation wins claims

The single biggest difference between fast claims and slow claims is pre-loss documentation. Operators who have:

  • An updated equipment schedule with serial numbers, year, hours, and current market value
  • Photos of each piece of equipment from multiple angles, dated within 12 months
  • A digital copy of every purchase invoice and finance contract
  • An inventory of small tools and equipment by truck

...settle their claims in days, not months. Operators who can't document tend to fight the carrier's adjuster on every line.

The storm-season checklist

  1. Pull the equipment schedule. Add anything missing. Update values.
  2. Photograph every unit and every loaded trailer. Email the photos to yourself.
  3. Identify open-ground staging at every job site at least 200 feet from tree lines.
  4. Confirm contractor's tools coverage on every service truck.
  5. Confirm trailer schedules (lowboys, floats, chip vans) are listed and rated.
  6. Confirm business-interruption waiting period and limit.
  7. Save claim hotline numbers and your agent's direct cell in your phone.
