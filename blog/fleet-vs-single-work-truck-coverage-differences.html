Fleet vs. Single Work Truck: Coverage Differences

A one-truck policy and a twenty-truck fleet policy look the same on the cover page. They behave differently the day a driver wrecks. Here's what changes as your operation grows.

February 12, 2026 8 min read Summerville, SC

The first work truck a small contractor buys usually lands on a basic commercial auto policy with a single named driver — the owner. Five trucks later, the policy that was perfect for a one-man shop is wasting money, slowing down hires, and creating coverage gaps every time a new employee gets behind the wheel.

The four-stage progression of commercial auto

Stage 1: Single vehicle, named-driver policy

One truck, one or two drivers explicitly listed on the schedule. Cheapest premium per vehicle. Every driver change requires a midterm endorsement. Best for: solo operators, sole-prop tradespeople with a stable driver list.

Stage 2: 2–4 vehicles, scheduled fleet

Each vehicle individually rated, drivers still scheduled, but priced as a multi-unit account. Eligible for safe-driver discounts and small multi-vehicle credits. Best for: contractors who haven't crossed the fleet threshold but need consolidated billing and one renewal.

Stage 3: 5+ vehicles, fleet policy with blanket drivers

Most carriers consider 5 or more "power units" a fleet. This unlocks meaningful changes:

  • Any-driver authorization — anyone with a valid CDL (or appropriate license) can operate any unit. New hire on Monday is covered Tuesday.
  • Fleet-rated premium — based on aggregate experience modifier rather than per-driver MVRs.
  • Fleet safety discounts — telematics, dashcams, written safety program, in-house training.
  • Consolidated claims handling — one adjuster team, one loss-run report, easier renewal underwriting.

Stage 4: 10+ vehicles, fleet symbol coverage

"Fleet symbols" (numbers 1–9 on a commercial auto policy) replace the schedule of vehicles with categories:

  • Symbol 1 — any auto (broadest)
  • Symbol 2 — owned autos only
  • Symbol 7 — specifically described autos
  • Symbol 8 — hired autos
  • Symbol 9 — non-owned autos

With Symbol 1 or 2, you can add and remove vehicles mid-term without endorsements — the policy adjusts at audit. For a growing fleet, this saves hours of back-and-forth and prevents uncovered vehicles.

What "any auto" really means

A general contractor in Berkeley County had a 12-truck fleet on Symbol 7 (specifically described autos). He bought a used skid-steer trailer at auction, hooked it to his pickup, and drove it home. Trailer was uncovered for 11 days because no one called the agent. Symbol 1 ("any auto") would have closed the gap automatically. Cost difference: about $400/year.

Where fleets save money

  • Telematics & dashcams — most carriers offer 5 – 15% off for fleet-wide installations. Lytx, Samsara, Motive, and KeepTruckin are all eligible.
  • Driver-screening discounts — written hiring criteria (MVR, CDL, age, experience) verified at renewal can knock 3 – 8% off.
  • Loss-control program — Swamp Fox includes a free loss-control review for fleet clients. Documented programs lower mod factors.
  • Higher deductibles — a fleet with cash flow can take a $5K or $10K physical-damage deductible and save 15 – 25% on premium.

Where fleets get stung

  • Inadequate UM/UIM limits across the fleet — easy to overlook, expensive at claim time.
  • Failing to schedule trailers — many fleet schedules cover power units but require trailers to be listed separately.
  • Hauling for hire vs. private carriage — if any unit is operating under USDOT authority, MCS-90 endorsement and federal filings are required. Mixing creates compliance issues.
  • Forgetting cargo coverage — auto liability doesn't pay for damaged freight.
Plan the transition before you cross the threshold

If you're at 4 trucks and growing, talk to us before the 5th unit goes on the road. Restructuring at fleet onset is faster, cleaner, and cheaper than retrofitting at renewal six months later.
