Commercial Auto

How to Insure Service Vans Without Costly Coverage Gaps

The plumbing van, the HVAC truck, the electrician's box-body Sprinter — they all carry a common problem. Personal auto won't pay if something goes wrong on the job, and the wrong commercial policy leaves $50,000 of tools and equipment uncovered.

April 2, 2026 7 min read Goose Creek, SC

Most service-business owners we meet started with a personal auto policy on their first work van. It was cheaper, the agent let it slide, and nothing ever happened. Then something does happen — a wreck, a stolen tool box, an employee crash in their own car — and they discover their policy was never built to respond.

Why personal auto fails the second you start working out of the van

Every personal auto policy on the market contains a "business use" exclusion. The fine print varies, but the intent is identical: the carrier did not price the policy for commercial risk and will deny the claim if the vehicle was being used for a business purpose at the time of loss. Driving to a job site, hauling tools, transporting a coworker — that's commercial use.

The exclusion isn't theoretical. We have watched it deny totaled-vehicle claims, third-party injury claims, and stolen-tool claims. The deck is stacked against the personal-policy holder the moment the claim notes mention "the customer."

The four-policy stack a service business actually needs

1. Commercial auto on the van itself

This is the foundation. It covers liability to other people, physical damage to your van (collision and comprehensive), medical payments, and uninsured-motorist coverage at limits priced for commercial use. Liability limits should start at $1M combined single limit for a single van — anything less and you risk being out-protected by the other driver's lawyer.

2. Hired & non-owned auto (HNOA)

The single most-overlooked endorsement in the service trades. HNOA covers the business when an employee is driving:

  • Their own personal vehicle on company business — running for parts, picking up materials, driving between job sites.
  • A rented vehicle — the U-Haul rented for the day, the rental car after a wreck.

Without HNOA, a wreck in an employee's personal car becomes a wreck where the business is named in the lawsuit and the business has no coverage. HNOA usually adds $150 – $400/year to a commercial auto policy.

3. Inland marine for tools and equipment

Standard commercial auto pays for the van. It does not pay for the $40,000 of tools, ladders, copper, and diagnostic equipment inside the van. That is "contractor's tools and equipment" coverage, written on an inland marine form. Get it scheduled (high-value items listed individually) plus a blanket limit for everything else.

4. General liability

This responds when the work itself causes harm — a misinstalled water heater that floods a customer's basement, an electrician who burns down a garage. Auto liability won't pay for that. More on general liability →

A real Goose Creek claim

An HVAC contractor's helper drove his own truck to a job after a service van broke down. He rear-ended a sedan on Red Bank Road. The sedan driver sued the contractor for $185,000 in injuries. The contractor's commercial auto policy didn't apply (employee's vehicle), and the helper's personal auto carried only $50,000 in liability. Without HNOA, the contractor was personally exposed for $135,000 over and above the helper's limit.

How much does the right policy actually cost?

For a single service van in Berkeley County with a clean MVR and $1M liability, expect $1,800 – $3,200/year for commercial auto, plus $150 – $400 for HNOA, plus $400 – $900 for $25,000 of contractor's tools coverage. That's roughly $2,400 – $4,500/year for the full stack. The first denied claim would cost more than five years of premium.

Bundling tip

If you carry a Business Owner's Policy (BOP) for general liability and property, most carriers will write the commercial auto on the same account at a 5 – 12% multi-policy discount and consolidate billing. Always worth asking.

This week's checklist

  1. Confirm every work vehicle is on a commercial auto policy, not a personal one.
  2. Verify HNOA is endorsed if any employee ever drives a personal or rented vehicle for work.
  3. Inventory your tools and equipment — write down brand, model, and replacement value. Schedule anything over $2,500 individually.
  4. Match titles, named insureds, and DBA names across every policy. One mismatch will burn you.
