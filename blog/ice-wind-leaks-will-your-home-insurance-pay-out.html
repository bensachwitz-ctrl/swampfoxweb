Ice, Wind, & Leaks: Will Your Home Insurance Pay Out?

A 60-mph gust strips the roof. A pine cracks and lands on the back porch. A pipe behind the laundry-room wall lets go at 2 a.m. The big question after a Lowcountry storm is always the same: does the policy pay?

March 19, 2026 8 min read Summerville, SC

Standard homeowners policies are written on what's called an HO-3 form, which covers your home on an "open perils" basis and your contents on a "named perils" basis. Translation: nearly anything can hit your house and be covered, but only specific things can hit your stuff. The pattern of what's in and out matters most after a storm.

What standard HO-3 covers, plain English

  • Wind damage to the roof, siding, gutters, and structure. Including shingles peeled by a 50 mph gust.
  • Fallen-tree damage — even your own tree falling on your own house. Removal of the tree from the structure is included; removal from the yard alone is usually not.
  • Sudden & accidental water damage — a burst pipe, a failed washing-machine hose, a frozen line that splits.
  • Fire, lightning, smoke, hail, and explosion.
  • Theft and vandalism.
  • Personal property at typically 50 – 70% of the dwelling limit.
  • Loss of use — hotel, meals, additional living expenses while your home is unlivable.
  • Personal liability — if a guest is injured at your home or your dog bites someone off-property.

The hurricane-deductible trap

South Carolina coastal counties — Charleston, Berkeley, Dorchester, and others — almost always have a separate named-storm or hurricane deductible, expressed as a percentage of dwelling coverage rather than a flat dollar amount. On a $400,000 home, a 2% hurricane deductible is $8,000 before the policy pays a dime.

Read your declarations page now

Look for "Wind/Hail Deductible" or "Named Storm Deductible." If it's expressed as a percentage (1%, 2%, 5%), multiply by your dwelling coverage to know your real out-of-pocket. Some homeowners save up an emergency fund just to cover this.

The first 24 hours after damage

  1. Photograph everything before cleanup. Wide shots, close-ups, every room. The carrier needs proof of pre-mitigation condition.
  2. Mitigate further damage. Tarp the roof, board the windows, dry the floors. The policy requires you to limit additional damage — and pays for reasonable mitigation expenses.
  3. Save receipts. Tarps, fans, dehumidifier rentals, hotel nights. All reimbursable under loss-of-use and mitigation provisions.
  4. Call us, not the carrier directly. We open the claim with the right adjuster team and stay involved through settlement.
  5. Don't sign anything from a "public adjuster" who knocks on your door. Read it carefully or call us before signing.

Three endorsements most Lowcountry homes need

1. Service-line coverage

Buried water, sewer, gas, and electrical lines from the street to your house. A tree root cracks the line and the repair excavation alone is $4,000 – $10,000. Endorsement runs $30 – $50/year. Worth it.

2. Equipment-breakdown coverage

HVAC, well pump, water heater, electrical panel — fails on its own (not from a covered peril). Standard policy excludes. Endorsement runs $25 – $50/year and pays for the unit and installation. Pays for itself the first time.

3. Replacement cost on contents

Without this, your 8-year-old TV is reimbursed at depreciated value (~$120 instead of the $800 to replace it). Replacement-cost endorsement on personal property typically adds 5 – 10% to premium and is one of the biggest claims-day differences you can buy.

Annual review checklist

Once a year, check three things: (1) is your dwelling limit at least replacement cost per current local construction estimates? (2) have you added contents (jewelry, art, tools) that need scheduling? (3) when was the last roof inspection — many carriers will non-renew on roofs older than 20 years.
